How to Use the Binomial Distribution Calculator

1. Introduction to Binomial Distribution

A binomial distribution describes the number of successess in a fixed number of trials, where each trial has two possible outcomes (success or failure), and the probability of success is constant across trials

Real World Example: If you flip a coin 10 times, the binomial distribution calculates the probability of getting exactly 3 heads (successess) out of those 10 flips

2. Key Terms in Binomial Distribution

Understanding the terms used in Binomial Distribution is crucial to understand how the calculator works. These are the key terms that are used in the calculator

  • X (Random Variable): It represents a random variable that counts the number of successes in a given number of trials. It can take integer values from 0 to n, where n is the total number of trials. For example, if you flip a coin 10 times, X could represent the number of heads that appear.

  • x (Number of Successes): It is a specific value that the random variable X can take. It is used to calculate the probability of having exactly x successes out of n trials. For example, if X represents the number of heads in 10 flips of a coin, x = 5 would mean you are interested in the probability of getting exactly 5 heads.

  • P (Probability): It represents the probability of specific events or outcomes.

  • p (Probability of Success): It is the probability of success on a single trial.

  • n (Number of Trials): It represents the number of trials or experiments.

3. Steps for Using the Calculator

  • Step 1. Enter the probability of success for each trial (between 0 and 1). The numbers 0 and 1 represent 0% and 100% respectively. So, if you have a fair coin, the probability of heads (success) is 0.5 (50%).
    The probability of success is represented by lowercase p

  • Step 2. Enter the number of trials or experiments you will conduct. For example, if you're flipping a coin 10 times, the number of trial is 10 times.
    The number of trials is represented by lowercase n

  • Step 3. Enter the number of successes you want to calculate the probability for. For example, if you're interested in how likely it is to get 3 heads out of 10 coin flips, enter 3
    The number of successes is represented by lowercase x
    Note that this is different from the variable X, which represents a random variable that counts the number of successes in a given number of trials.

  • Step 4. After entering your values, click the "Calculate" button to compute the binomial and cummulative probabilities

Note that the number of successes cannot exceed the number of trials. Doing so will result in an undefined value.

4. Understanding the Output

  • Binomial Probability: P(X = x)
    The binomial probability shows the likelihood of getting exactly x successes in n trials

  • Cumulative Probability: P(X < x)
    The cumulative probability P(X < x) represents the probability of getting fewer than x successes in n trials

  • Cumulative Probability P(X ≤ x)
    The probability of getting x or fewer successes in n trials

  • Cumulative Probability P(X > x)
    The probability of getting more than x successes in n trials

  • Cumulative Probability P(X ≥ x)
    The probability of getting x or more successes in n trials

5. Sample Problems

  • Suppose a basketball player makes 60% of free throws. He attempts 10 free throws. What is the probability that he will make exactly 7 of them?

    • p (Probability of Success): 0.6 (60%)
    • n (Number of Trials): 10
    • x (Number of Successes): 7

    The one we are looking for is the Binomial Probability: P(X = x), where the number of successes must be exactly 7 out of the 10 total trials. Assign the values above to the calculator and check the results!

  • In a survey, 70% of people say they like chocolate. Suppose we randomly select 12 people. What is the probability that more than 7 of them will say they like chocolate?

    • p (Probability of Success): 0.7
    • n (Number of Trials): 12
    • x (Number of Successes): 7

    The one we are looking for is the Cumulative Probability: P(X > x), where the number of successes must be more than 7 out of the 12 total trials. Assign the values above to the calculator and check the results!

  • The College of Engineering at LSPU-SCC had an overall passing rate of 67.31%. If a group of 15 engineering graduates from this college takes the licensure exam, what is the probability that 8 or less of them will pass?

    • p (Probability of Success): 0.6731
    • n (Number of Trials): 15
    • x (Number of Successes): 8

    The one we are looking for is the Cumulative Probability: P(X ≤ x), where the number of successes must be 8 or less out of the 15 total trials. Assign the values above to the calculator and check the results!
